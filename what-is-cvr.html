What is CVR?

Content Validity Ratio formula, Lawshe's thresholds, practical calculation, and interpretation

CVR (Content Validity Ratio) is a statistical measure used in scale development and instrument validation to evaluate how well individual items on a measurement instrument represent the intended construct. Developed by C. H. Lawshe in 1975, CVR quantifies expert panel agreement about whether each item is "essential" to include on the scale.

CVR is commonly used in:

  • Developing and validating questionnaires and surveys
  • Creating competency assessment tools
  • Building certification exam content
  • Evaluating educational curriculum relevance
  • Assessing job task lists and performance indicators

CVR Formula

CVR = (ne − N/2) / (N/2)

ne: Number of experts rating the item as "essential"
N: Total number of experts rating the item

This formula produces values between −1 and +1:

  • +1: All experts agree the item is essential (perfect agreement)
  • 0 to +0.99: Majority of experts agree it's essential (partial agreement)
  • 0: Exactly half the experts say essential, half say not (no consensus)
  • −1 to 0: Majority of experts say not essential (low relevance)
  • −1: No experts consider it essential (should be removed)

Lawshe's Minimum CVR Thresholds

For an item to be considered valid, its CVR must meet or exceed the minimum threshold corresponding to the number of experts on the panel. These thresholds ensure that the agreement is statistically significant at the p < 0.05 level:

Why These Thresholds?

When you have fewer experts (5-7), the threshold is very high (0.99) because you need nearly perfect agreement. As the expert panel grows, the threshold drops because statistical significance is easier to achieve with more data points. With 40 experts, even 0.29 is considered statistically significant agreement.

Practical Example

Scenario: Testing a new survey

Situation: A research team develops a 10-item depression scale and asks 10 experts to rate how "essential" each item is using a 3-point Lawshe scale (1=Essential, 2=Useful but not essential, 3=Not necessary).

Item 1 Results:

  • 8 experts rate it as "Essential" (1)
  • 2 experts rate it as "Useful but not essential" (2 or 3)

Calculation for Item 1:

CVR = (8 − 10/2) / (10/2) = (8 − 5) / 5 = 3 / 5 = 0.60

Interpretation: With 10 experts, the minimum CVR threshold is 0.62. This item's CVR of 0.60 falls below the cutoff, so it should be revised or removed from the scale.

What if 9 experts had rated it essential?

CVR = (9 − 5) / 5 = 4 / 5 = 0.80 ✓ Valid

Interpretation Guide

Valid Items (CVR ≥ Threshold)

Items with CVR values meeting or exceeding the threshold demonstrate significant expert agreement. These items should be retained on the scale.

Items Needing Revision (CVR just below Threshold)

If an item is close to the threshold, consider:

  • Rewording the item for clarity
  • Consulting with experts who disagreed about why
  • Gathering feedback on how to improve it

Invalid Items (CVR significantly below Threshold)

Items with very low CVR values (e.g., 0.20 or lower) should be removed entirely, as they lack expert consensus.

Key Advantages of CVR

  • Simplicity: Easy to understand and calculate
  • Quantifies consensus: Provides a single number representing expert agreement
  • Widely accepted: Standard method in scale development research
  • Flexible: Works with any number of experts (5+)
  • Statistically sound: Thresholds are based on statistical significance tests

Limitations

  • Doesn't account for intensity: Treats a rating of "Essential" the same whether given by 5 or 100 experts
  • Requires adequate expert panel: Minimum 5 experts recommended
  • Only item-level: Doesn't provide a single scale-level validity score (use CVI or S-CVI/Ave for that)
